Dive Smarter, Thrive Deeper

By YODO Diver April 7, 2025
3 mins read

A Beginner's Thrilling Leap into the Deep

I'll never forget the rush of excitement I felt when I first stepped into the world of scuba diving. It all started during a trip to the east coast of Malaysia with friends, where we stayed at a stunning resort in Pulau Perhentian. A PADI Dive Center on-site caught our attention, and since one of my friends was a certified Open Water Diver eager to log more dives, they invited me and another friend to try a Discover Scuba Diving experience—a perfect introduction for beginners. The instructor's warm welcome set the tone for an adventure I couldn't wait to dive into. After a 30-minute briefing on the basics—hand signals, continuous breathing through the regulator, clearing water from our masks, and recovering the mouthpiece—we were ready. Fins in hand, we walked barefoot to the water's edge, slipped them on, and waded in. Just a short swim from the dive center, we descended to about 8 meters. That first dive was magical: I swam among anemones with clownfish (Nemo!), spotted two small whitetip reef sharks, saw stingrays resting on the sandy ocean floor, and marveled at schools of silvery, shiny fish darting around, their names a mystery to me. The 30-minute dive felt like stepping into another world, and I was absolutely hooked.

Diving Deeper, Eyes Wide Open

That same day, the instructor noticed my excitement and invited me to join my friend for an afternoon dive. We took a boat to a deeper site, descending to 12-15 meters. The underwater world expanded before me, revealing creatures I couldn't even name—their vibrant colors and strange shapes left me in awe. Back at the resort, we spent time relaxing with the dive instructors, who shared stories of coral restoration, diving tips, and their own adventures. Their passion was infectious, planting a seed for more underwater exploration. My friend and I, already planning a trip to Bali in a few months, decided we'd dive there too. Little did I know how those early dives would shape my journey—and how much I still had to learn about diving safely.

Facing the Challenges of Nusa Penida

In Bali, diving became a highlight of our trip, especially since it was the day before my birthday. I did three dives in Nusa Penida, reaching depths of 18-20 meters, but the conditions tested my limits. The first dive was exhausting—it felt like a cardio workout as we had to backswim against strong currents on the surface before descending. Underwater, I spotted a triggerfish but didn't dare get close, wary of its reputation for being territorial. The second dive brought a breathtaking reward: 8-12 giant manta rays, their massive wingspans stretching wider than five humans side by side. I was in awe of their sheer size. The third dive was a visual feast, with vibrant green and red corals that reminded me of a Christmas coral village, glowing with festive beauty. But the dives were tough—cold water, strong currents, and choppy waves made swimming a challenge. I hadn't practiced buoyancy control much, so I struggled, relying on my hands to swim against the currents instead of proper finning techniques. As an uncertified diver, I didn't fully grasp the risks I was taking, too caught up in the thrill to realize the potential dangers.

A Wake-Up Call Through Certification

Months later, I moved back to the Philippines and joined a friend for a freediving trip. Freediving was a different beast—more challenging with its focus on breath-holding and ear equalization. I initially planned to stick to freediving, but the dive center offered PADI Open Water certification. During a break, I reflected on my past dives and decided to pursue it. The next day, a Japanese instructor arrived, and I began my training. I studied a handbook, watched videos, and practiced drills: breathing techniques, maintaining neutral buoyancy, equalizing ear pressure, safety protocols, hand signals, and equipment checks. Over two days, I earned my certification, and it was a game-changer. One of the coaches noticed my ease in the water and asked if I'd dived before. I shared my experiences in Malaysia and Indonesia, and he pointed out something sobering: uncertified divers shouldn't be diving to 18-20 meters, especially in challenging conditions. The risks—like decompression sickness (DCS) from ascending too quickly—were real. Safety was their priority, and I realized how much I'd unknowingly put myself at risk.

Swimming with Confidence and Wonder

The certification culminated in a “fun dive” to 18-20 meters, and it was my best dive yet. This time, I wasn't panicking near corals, worried I'd damage them, or clinging to my dive buddies if I drifted slightly away. With proper training, I used my fins to navigate, controlled my breathing to ascend and descend smoothly, and maintained neutral buoyancy. I understood the science behind the movements, and that knowledge made all the difference. I swam alongside a sea turtle, glided through a massive school of fish, and spotted an array of underwater life: playful clownfish (Nemos) that seemed to dance for my camera as if they knew they were being filmed, vibrant starfish, a striking lionfish, long needlefish (whose name I later learned), and a curious pufferfish. It wasn't just the calm waters that made this dive the best—it was my newfound confidence and understanding. The more you learn about scuba diving, the more you can let go of fear, move efficiently, and focus on the beauty around you. Now, I can't wait for my next dive, ready to explore more waters and hone my skills with every adventure.
